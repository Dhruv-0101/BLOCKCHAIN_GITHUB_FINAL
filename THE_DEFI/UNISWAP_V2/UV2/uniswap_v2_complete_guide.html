PART 00

What is Uniswap V2? Foundation

x · y = k
x = Token A reserve  |  y = Token B reserve  |  k = constant

Uniswap V2 = Automated Market Maker (AMM) — a decentralized exchange protocol that replaces the traditional order book model with pure mathematics. No order book. No buyer-seller matching. Just math.

❌ What it is NOT
  • No order book
  • No buyer-seller matching
  • No centralized intermediary
  • No price discovery by bids/asks
✅ What it IS
  • Pure math-based pricing
  • Constant product formula (x·y=k)
  • Permissionless liquidity pools
  • Anyone can trade 24/7 on-chain
Real-Life Analogy — The Fruit Market

Think of Uniswap as a fruit market (mandi). The pool is the shop, tokens are the fruits, and the formula is the pricing rule of the shop owner.

Pool = Fruit Market 🏪 Apple = Token A Orange = Token B ETH = Cash WETH = Tokenized Cash (ERC-20 version of ETH)

When you want oranges, you bring apples. The more apples you bring, the less oranges per apple you receive (price impact). That's the constant product curve in action.

Why x·y=k Works

Imagine the pool has 100 apples and 200 oranges → k = 20,000. If you want to buy 10 apples, the pool now has 90 apples. So new orange count = 20,000 / 90 = 222.2. You pay 22.2 oranges for 10 apples. The price moved because the ratio changed — that's slippage, and it's by design.

Before: Apple=100, Orange=200, k=20,000 You buy 10 Apple New Apple = 100 - 10 = 90 New Orange = 20,000 / 90 = 222.2 You paid = 222.2 - 200 = 22.2 Orange (for 10 Apple) Effective price = 2.22 Orange/Apple (vs 2.00 spot) // That extra 0.22 is your price impact / slippage

The Factory's sole job is to create trading pair contracts and maintain a registry. Think of it as the "Plot Registrar" — it doesn't trade itself, it just opens new shops.

  • Token order doesn't matter: Factory(A,B) = Factory(B,A) → same pair

PART 02

ETH / WETH — Complete Clarity

Why is ETH a Problem?

Uniswap pools work with ERC-20 token pairs only — every pool is ERC-20 + ERC-20. But ETH is NOT an ERC-20 token. It's the native coin of Ethereum. That means ETH can't be approve()d or transferFrom()d like a regular token.

❌ ETH Problems
  • Not an ERC-20 token
  • Cannot be approved
  • Cannot transferFrom
  • Pools require ERC-20 + ERC-20
✅ WETH Solution
  • WETH = Wrapped ETH
  • Full ERC-20 compatibility
  • Always 1:1 with ETH
  • No price difference ever
Router's Magic — Automatic Wrapping

The Router makes this seamless. When you send ETH, the Router automatically wraps it to WETH before interacting with the pool. When you receive ETH back, it unwraps WETH for you.

ETH coming in? → Router wraps: ETH → WETH → WETH enters the pool WETH going out? → Router unwraps: WETH → ETH → You receive native ETH

✅ As a developer or user, you can completely ignore WETH's existence as long as you use the Router. It handles everything behind the scenes.

PART 03

Creating a Pair

Creating a pair is like opening a new shop in the market. You need two tokens (Apple + Orange), and you call the Factory to register this new trading venue. This only happens once per token combination — ever.

// Solidity — Create a new trading pair factory.createPair(address tokenA, address tokenB); // Real example: Create Apple/Orange pair factory.createPair(APPLE_TOKEN, ORANGE_TOKEN); // Returns the new pair's contract address
⭐

createPair() is called only once. After that, the pair exists forever on-chain and anyone can provide liquidity or trade on it. The Factory ensures no duplicate pairs can be created.

What Happens Under the Hood
  1. You call factory.createPair(tokenA, tokenB)
  2. Factory deploys a new UniswapV2Pair contract
  3. Factory stores the mapping: (tokenA, tokenB) → pair address
  4. The new pair is initialized with zero reserves
  5. First liquidity provider sets the initial price ratio

Case 1: ERC-20 + ERC-20

This is the standard case — adding two regular ERC-20 tokens as liquidity. You become a Liquidity Provider (LP) and receive LP tokens as proof of your ownership share.

When you want to add ETH (not WETH) as one of the tokens. You use addLiquidityETH() and send ETH as the transaction value. The Router wraps it automatically.

  1. User sends ETH with the transaction (msg.value)

What is an LP Token?

LP (Liquidity Provider) tokens are ERC-20 tokens issued by the Pair contract. They represent your proportional share of the pool reserves. Think of them as a claim check at a coat room — you show them later to get your tokens back.

LP Token = Ownership
  • Proportional pool share
  • Accrues trading fees (0.3%)
  • Transferable / tradeable
  • Burned when you remove liquidity
LP ≠ HODL
  • Subject to impermanent loss
  • Value tied to pool ratio
  • Not the same as just holding
  • Fees may or may not offset IL
PART 05

Remove Liquidity

Removing liquidity is the reverse of adding it. You hand back your LP tokens (the "receipt") and get your underlying tokens back — proportional to your share of the pool at that moment.

🏪 Real-life analogy: Show your LP receipt → Get your fruit back. "LP receipt dikhao, fruit wapas le jao."

solidity
// Remove ERC-20 + ERC-20 liquidity router.removeLiquidity( address tokenA, address tokenB, uint liquidity, // LP token amount to burn uint amountAMin, // Min tokens you accept (slippage guard) uint amountBMin, address to, uint deadline ); // Remove ETH + ERC-20 liquidity router.removeLiquidityETH(...); // Automatically unwraps WETH → ETH for you
Step-by-Step Flow
  1. Approve LP tokens — call lpToken.approve(router, amount)
  2. Router burns LP tokens — sends them to the Pair, which burns them
  3. Tokens returned — Pair sends back your proportional share of reserves
  4. For ETH variant — Router automatically unwraps WETH back to ETH
⚠️

The tokens you receive may NOT be the same amounts you deposited. Pool ratios change over time as trades happen. You receive your share of the current pool, not your original deposit amounts.

Swaps — Heart of Uniswap

Uniswap V2 exposes 4 swap functions depending on what you're trading (ETH vs ERC-20) and which side of the trade you want exact control over (input vs output).

CASE 1 swapExactTokensForTokens Most Common

Meaning: "I'm giving you an exact amount of Token A. Give me however many Token B that works out to — just not less than my minimum."

🍎 "Main exact Apple de raha hoon, jitne Orange milenge woh chahiye" — I give exact Apple, get however many Orange comes out.

solidity
router.swapExactTokensForTokens( uint amountIn, // Exact input amount (you control this) uint amountOutMin, // Minimum output (slippage guard) address[] path, // [tokenIn, tokenOut] or multi-hop address to, // Who receives the output tokens uint deadline // Transaction must complete by this time );
Math Example (Pool: Apple=100, Orange=200, k=20,000)
User wants to swap 10 Apple New Apple reserve = 100 + 10 = 110 New Orange reserve = 20,000 / 110 = 181.8 Orange out = 200 - 181.8 = 18.2 Orange (Before 0.3% fee deduction) Effective rate: 1 Apple ≈ 1.82 Orange (vs 2.0 spot — price impact!)
CASE 2 swapTokensForExactTokens

Meaning: "I want exactly X amount of Token B. Take as many Token A as you need — but don't take more than my maximum."

🍊 "Mujhe exact Orange chahiye, max Apple main dunga" — I want exact Orange, I'll give up to max Apple for it.

solidity
router.swapTokensForExactTokens( uint amountOut, // Exact output you want (you control this) uint amountInMax, // Max input you'll spend (slippage guard) address[] path, address to, uint deadline );
CASE 3 swapExactETHForTokens

Meaning: "I'm sending exact ETH. Give me tokens in return." The Router wraps ETH → WETH internally, then executes the swap.

⚡ "Main ETH de raha hoon, token chahiye" — Router handles: ETH → WETH → swap → Token

solidity
router.swapExactETHForTokens{value: msg.value}( uint amountOutMin, address[] path, // path[0] must be WETH address address to, uint deadline ); // Router internally: ETH → wrap → WETH → swap → Token
CASE 4 swapExactTokensForETH

Meaning: "I'm giving exact tokens. Give me ETH back." Router executes the swap then unwraps WETH → ETH for you.

💰 "Token de raha hoon, ETH chahiye" — Router handles: Token → swap → WETH → unwrap → ETH

PART 07

What is Path?

The path is an array of token addresses that defines the route a swap takes. It tells the Router: "Go from Token A → Token B → Token C, using each adjacent pool along the way."

address[] path; // path[0] = token you're selling // path[last] = token you're receiving // everything in between = intermediate hops
Types of Paths
Simple Swap (2 hops)
Apple → Orange path = [Apple, Orange]

Direct pool: Apple/Orange pair

ETH Swap
ETH → Apple path = [WETH, Apple]

Note: ETH is represented as WETH in path

Multi-Hop Swap (3+ tokens)
Apple → Orange → Banana path = [Apple, Orange, Banana] // Real example: SHIB → ETH → USDC // (No direct SHIB/USDC pool exists, // but SHIB/ETH and ETH/USDC both exist) path = [SHIB, WETH, USDC]

Each adjacent pair in the path must have an existing liquidity pool. The Router automatically routes through multiple pools in a single transaction.

🧭 Router automatically chooses the best path when you use off-chain helpers. For on-chain calls, you specify the path explicitly. Multi-hop enables trading any token for any other token, even without a direct pool.

PART 08

Fees & Slippage

The 0.3% Fee

Every swap in Uniswap V2 charges a flat 0.3% fee on the input amount. This fee is NOT taken out separately — it stays in the pool and automatically increases the reserves, effectively distributing it proportionally to all LP token holders.

Fee = 0.3%
Per swap • Automatically added to pool reserves • LP holders earn pro-rata
You swap 100 Apple: Fee = 100 × 0.003 = 0.3 Apple stays in pool Effective input = 99.7 Apple for price calculation After many trades, pool reserves grow beyond initial k value → LP holders can claim this growth when removing liquidity
Slippage Protection — amountOutMin

Slippage is the difference between the expected price and the actual execution price. Between when you submit a transaction and when it executes on-chain, other trades can move the price.

amountOutMin is your safety net. If the actual output falls below this threshold (due to price movement or sandwich attacks), the transaction reverts — protecting your funds.

✅ With Slippage Protection
amountOutMin = expectedOut × 0.99 // Max 1% slippage tolerance If price moves > 1%: → Transaction REVERTS ✓ → Your tokens are safe ✓
❌ Without Protection (amountOutMin=0)
amountOutMin = 0 // Accept any output — DANGEROUS Sandwich attacker: → Buys before you (moves price) → Your trade executes at bad price → Attacker sells after you
⚠️

Always set a reasonable amountOutMin. Setting it to 0 makes you vulnerable to MEV bots and sandwich attacks. Typical safe values: 0.5% to 2% slippage tolerance.

PART 09

Impermanent Loss — The LP Risk

Impermanent Loss (IL) is the temporary loss experienced by liquidity providers when the price of deposited tokens changes relative to when they were deposited. The bigger the price divergence, the larger the IL.

📊

LP ≠ HODL. Being an LP is not the same as just holding your tokens. If the token prices move significantly in one direction, you'd have been better off just holding.

LP Earns
  • 0.3% fee from every trader swap
  • Proportional to pool share
  • Fees auto-compound in pool
LP Risks
  • Price movement causes IL
  • Greater divergence = more IL
  • 2x price move → ~5.7% IL
  • 5x price move → ~25.5% IL
Why "Impermanent"?

It's called impermanent because if prices return to the original ratio when you exit, the loss disappears. It only becomes permanent when you actually remove liquidity while prices are diverged. If fees earned > IL experienced, LPs still profit overall.

⚡ Developer perspective: As a developer integrating Uniswap, you don't implement IL — it's automatic. Just know it exists so you can warn users who are providing liquidity.

FLASH SWAP

Flash Swaps — DeFi Magic

⚡ One-Line Intuition
Take tokens from the pool WITHOUT paying first → use them → pay back WITH fee → all in one transaction. If you don't pay back → EVERYTHING REVERTS as if it never happened.
Real-World Analogy — The Shop Deal
🏪 Scenario: You're a DeFi trader

You notice: Uniswap sells iPhone for ₹1000, SushiSwap sells iPhone for ₹1100. But you have ₹0.

Normal world: "No money → can't trade" ❌ Uniswap V2 says: "Take the iPhone, sell it for profit, give me my money + fee at the end." Steps: 1. Borrow iPhone from Uniswap (free for now) 2. Sell iPhone on SushiSwap for ₹1100 3. Pay Uniswap back ₹1000 + 0.3% fee 4. Keep ₹97 profit 💰 All in ONE transaction.
Blockchain DeFi Version — With Real Numbers

You spot an arbitrage: Uniswap ETH/DAI pool has 1 ETH = 2000 DAI. SushiSwap ETH/DAI pool has 1 ETH = 2050 DAI. ETH is cheaper on Uniswap!

Market prices: Uniswap: 1 ETH = 2,000 DAI (cheap) SushiSwap: 1 ETH = 2,050 DAI (expensive) Opportunity: Buy ETH cheap on Uni, sell expensive on Sushi
STEP 1 — Call Uniswap
You call pair.swap(10 ETH, 0 DAI, myContract, data). The non-empty data field tells Uniswap this is a flash swap. You haven't paid anything yet.
STEP 2 — Uniswap Sends ETH First 😱
Uniswap V2's rule: "If data is non-empty, I send tokens first." So Uniswap sends you 10 ETH immediately. You now have 10 real ETH — but haven't paid anything yet!
STEP 3 — Callback Function
Uniswap calls uniswapV2Call(sender, amount0, amount1, data) on your contract. This is where the magic happens — your code runs.
STEP 4 — Arbitrage
Inside the callback: sell 10 ETH on SushiSwap. You get 10 × 2050 = 20,500 DAI.
STEP 5 — Calculate Payback
Uniswap wants back: 10 ETH + 0.3% fee = 10.03 ETH. At 2000 DAI/ETH, that's 10.03 × 2000 = 20,060 DAI.
STEP 6 — Payback + Profit ✅
You pay Uniswap 20,060 DAI. You keep: 20,500 - 20,060 = 440 DAI profit! Transaction ends successfully.
🎯 The Profit Math
SushiSwap sale: 10 ETH × 2050 = 20,500 DAI received Uniswap payback: 10.03 ETH × 2000 = 20,060 DAI paid Net Profit: 20,500 - 20,060 = 440 DAI 🎉 You started with: 0 money You ended with: 440 DAI Risk taken: 0 (atomic transaction)
What Happens If You Don't Pay Back?
Suppose you fail: SushiSwap price changed → you couldn't make profit You couldn't afford to pay Uniswap back Uniswap checks at transaction end: "Did I get my money + fee? NO ❌" → Transaction REVERT Result: - You never received ETH (it was never real) - You have no loss - Uniswap has no loss - Everything is as if nothing happened - Gas fee is wasted, that's it
🛡️

Ethereum transactions are ATOMIC — either ALL steps succeed, or ALL steps fail. There's no partial execution. This is why flash swaps are safe for both parties.

Why Uniswap Allows This — Atomicity

Ethereum transactions are atomic — they're indivisible units. Either every state change succeeds, or none of them persist. Uniswap exploits this property:

Technical Rule (Uniswap V2 Pair says)

"At the END of swap(), either the reserve balance must be correct (accounting for fee), or the transaction reverts." — This is checked after your callback runs, making flash swaps safe.

Flash Swap Use Cases
  1. Arbitrage — Price difference exploitation between DEXes
  2. Collateral Swap — Switch collateral types in Aave/Compound without needing capital
  3. Liquidations — Liquidate undercollateralized positions using borrowed funds
  4. Debt Refinancing — Move debt from one protocol to another in one tx

The Core Insight — One Transaction, Multiple Protocols

Uniswap and SushiSwap are NOT connected to each other. They have different contracts, different addresses, different code, different teams. Only Ethereum blockchain connects them. Yet your contract can call BOTH in one transaction — because one Ethereum transaction can call any number of smart contracts sequentially.

Think of it like a phone call where you conference in Zomato, Uber, and your Bank — all in one call. If any call fails, everything is cancelled.

// Your arbitrage contract — calls BOTH protocols in one transaction function execute() external { // 1. Get ETH from Uniswap (flash swap) uniswapPair.swap(...); // 2. Sell ETH on SushiSwap for profit sushiRouter.swapExactTokensForTokens(...); // 3. Pay back Uniswap (with fee) token.transfer(uniswapPair, amountOwed); } // All 3 steps = ONE function call = ONE Ethereum transaction
⚡

Final Feel Statement: In DeFi, you don't "connect" systems. You call their already-deployed smart contracts via function calls. Your contract orchestrates everything. Ethereum ensures all-or-nothing execution.

DeFi is a LEGO System

Every protocol is a LEGO block. Uniswap, SushiSwap, Curve, Aave — all are already-deployed smart contracts with public addresses and public functions. You just call their functions from your contract. Like calling a library function in JavaScript. You don't copy their code. You just use their contract addresses + interfaces.

TWAP ORACLE

Price Oracles — TWAP Explained

Why Do Smart Contracts Need Prices?

Smart contracts are completely isolated from the real world — they can't Google a price, can't access the internet, can't call APIs. Yet many DeFi protocols desperately need reliable price data:

  • Lending apps (like Aave): "What is ETH worth right now for collateral checks?"
  • Stablecoins: "What is the collateral price to maintain peg?"
  • Prediction markets and options protocols
  • Liquidation bots: "Is this position underwater?"

⚠️ Smart contracts cannot access the internet. They need on-chain price sources — systems that store price data directly on the blockchain. These are called ORACLES.

Types of Oracles
Chainlink (Off-chain reporters)

Trusted reporters submit prices on-chain. High trust, some centralization. Used by most major DeFi protocols.

Uniswap TWAP (On-chain, AMM-based)

Derived purely from on-chain trading activity. Fully decentralized. But can be manipulated if pool liquidity is low.

The Naive Idea — And Why It's Wrong

The obvious approach: just read the current price from the pool.

// Naive approach: instant/spot price price = tokenB_reserve / tokenA_reserve Example: ETH = 10 (in pool) DAI = 20,000 (in pool) Spot price: 1 ETH = 2,000 DAI ← looks good!
⚠️

Problem: Spot price is easily manipulated! A large trade can dramatically move the price for just one block, allowing an attacker to drain a protocol that trusts the spot price.

// Real attack scenario Normal pool: ETH = 10, DAI = 20,000 → Price: 1 ETH = 2,000 DAI Attacker buys ETH with 18,000 DAI: Pool becomes: ETH ≈ 5, DAI ≈ 38,000 New spot price: 1 ETH ≈ 38,000 / 5 = 7,600 DAI (!!) This 7600 price lasts only for 1 block. But if a lending contract trusts it → PROTOCOL DRAINED ❌
TWAP — The Solution

💡 Core Idea of TWAP (Time-Weighted Average Price): Don't look at one instant. Average the price across TIME. A 1-block manipulation has almost no effect on a 30-minute average.

TWAP stands for Time-Weighted Average Price. Instead of reading the current price, it averages price over a time window — making manipulation prohibitively expensive.

Real-Life Analogy — Petrol Station
🚗 Petrol Price Example
Petrol prices today: For 1 hour (10am-11am): ₹100/litre (normal) For 1 minute (11am): ₹300/litre (glitch/spike!) For 9 hours (11am-8pm): ₹100/litre (back to normal) Someone asks: "What was today's petrol price?" ❌ Wrong answer: ₹300 (the spike) ✅ Right answer: ₹102 (the time-weighted average) TWAP = the daily average petrol price, not the spike.
How Uniswap V2 Implements TWAP

Uniswap V2 does NOT store the spot price. Instead, it stores price × time (priceCumulative). Every block, it adds (current price × seconds elapsed) to a running total. This is called the price accumulator.

// Uniswap V2 stores these in the Pair contract: uint224 price0CumulativeLast; // Token1/Token0 cumulative uint224 price1CumulativeLast; // Token0/Token1 cumulative uint32 blockTimestampLast; // Last update timestamp // Each block, before any swap, Uniswap updates: priceCumulative += (reserve_B / reserve_A) × timeElapsed
Working Through the Math
📊 TWAP Calculation Example
Start: Time = 0, priceCumulative = 0 For 10 minutes at normal price (2000 DAI/ETH): Contribution = 2000 × 10 = 20,000 priceCumulative = 20,000 Attack! 1 minute at spike price (7600 DAI/ETH): Contribution = 7600 × 1 = 7,600 priceCumulative = 20,000 + 7,600 = 27,600 Back to normal for 9 minutes: Contribution = 2000 × 9 = 18,000 priceCumulative = 27,600 + 18,000 = 45,600 TWAP calculation: Total time = 10 + 1 + 9 = 20 minutes TWAP = 45,600 / 20 = 2,280 DAI/ETH Spot price (manipulated): 7,600 ❌ TWAP price (safe): 2,280 ✅ (barely moved!) Attack FAILED. 🛡️
How Developers Use TWAP in Smart Contracts
// Developer reads TWAP in 3 steps: // Step 1: Read at time T1 (e.g., 30 minutes ago) uint priceCumulative1 = pair.price0CumulativeLast; uint32 timestamp1 = pair.blockTimestampLast; // Step 2: Read at time T2 (now) uint priceCumulative2 = pair.price0CumulativeLast; uint32 timestamp2 = uint32(block.timestamp); // Step 3: Calculate TWAP uint timeElapsed = timestamp2 - timestamp1; uint twap = (priceCumulative2 - priceCumulative1) / timeElapsed; // twap is now the average price over that period
Why is TWAP Manipulation Expensive?

To manipulate the TWAP, an attacker must maintain a fake price for many blocks. Each block they do this:

  • They must hold a large position (capital requirement)
  • Arbitrageurs drain them with every block (they lose money)
  • They pay 0.3% fees per block on each arbitrage trade
  • The longer they hold the fake price, the more they lose

💎 Result: TWAP manipulation is economically irrational. The attacker would need to sustain losses much larger than any potential gain from the manipulation.

TWAP Limitations (Honest Talk)
⚠️ TWAP is Not Perfect
  • Not real-time — always lags behind actual price
  • Short window = less safe — easier to manipulate
  • Long window = slow to respond — misses real price moves
  • Low liquidity pools — easier and cheaper to manipulate
Use Long TWAP For:
  • Lending protocols (Aave-style)
  • Collateral valuation
  • Governance decisions
  • Any high-stakes price check
Use Spot Price For:
  • Trading UI display
  • Quick price references
  • Low-stakes decisions
  • AMM routing calculations
Final TWAP Summary
  1. Smart contracts need on-chain price data → oracles
  2. Spot price = easily manipulated with one large trade ❌
  3. Uniswap V2 stores price × time (priceCumulative) every block
  4. TWAP = average price over a time window
  5. Short price spikes barely affect the long-term average
  6. Attacker must sustain fake price for many blocks → huge cost
  7. Result: Practically manipulation-resistant oracle ✅
